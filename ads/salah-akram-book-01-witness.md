**"This is not just a book; it is a screaming recorded on paper."**

Salah Akram wrote _Between Life and Death in Gaza_ from inside the genocide. Not looking back at it. Not interpreting it from a distance. Inside it.

He was a young man who wanted to build a future — university, football, late-night programming sessions in Rafah. Then October 7th happened, and then everything that came after, and then June 29th when he lost his uncle Saeed, and then the loss of his home, and then his friends — Mohammed, Abdullah, Ahmed, Ibrahim — one after another.

He writes: _"I write this not only as someone who lost his home, his uncle Saeed, and his closest friends — but as a witness bearing the weight of a shared wound. I write for the millions of displaced people, for the starving children, and for the families who watched their world vanish into smoke and dust."_

119 pages. Every one of them costs something to read.

This is a memoir. A testimony. A record that the people the bombs were aimed at were people — with names, with dreams, with programming sessions and football matches and uncles they loved.
